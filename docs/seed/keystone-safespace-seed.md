## 3. Charter draft (client-facing prose, voice-checked)

### Why this engagement exists
The first ten years were liftoff. SafeSpace built a fundraising engine, a real financial picture, an active board, deep roots in San Mateo County, and impact in the lives of young people through Campus, Community, and the Youth Action Board. The next ten are about sustainability: an organization rooted in this community and built to stay. This engagement is that investment. It is usually not the heart. It is the system.

### What we are building
Over six months, Sobo Consulting brings SafeSpace's systems into one customized hub, builds a full fundraising strategy with the weekly rhythms to run it, and develops Aris and Jasmine to lead fundraising and operate like nonprofit executives. Fundraising first. The board joins the fundraising work deliberately: a board fundraising toolkit and up to two working sessions so members can identify connections, make introductions, and support donor cultivation, with no fundraising targets or quotas for any director, and participation at each director's discretion.

### Where this ends (the six outcomes)
1. A full fundraising strategy, the internal rhythms to run it, and a digital tool built around how SafeSpace operates, from prospecting through cultivation, solicitation, and stewardship, with a major donor component covering prospect identification, cultivation, and solicitation.
2. The financial picture in a live dashboard: budget against actuals and runway, grounded in a three-year operating budget and an annual budget built with the team, with a board login and views the board controls.
3. Compliance streamlined and solid: a compliance calendar covering filings, registrations, renewals, and board governance dates, a chart of accounts review, and the core policies fundraising depends on (gift acceptance, expense, document retention), with the protections that come with working with minors.
4. Operating rhythms that hold the week, the month, and the quarter.
5. Aris and Jasmine running all of it with confidence and without us, fully onboarded into the hub.
6. An impact and evaluation framework and the funder-ready artifacts to track and showcase it.

### How we will work
Two modes at once: develop the people and build the system, then set it up to last. Five steps: Diagnose, Design, Build, Train, Stabilize. Every session is a working session with homework before and after, always tied to a real fundraising task. Each session adds one piece of the system, so by the end Aris and Jasmine are running something they built. Ownership transfers as they build it, not in one handoff at the end. Plan on four to six hours a week from each coachee: the session, the homework, and implementation time. Expect six to eight in the first month, when sessions run twice a week and the work is front-loaded. Treat it like training. These are estimates for planning, not requirements.

### What you own at the end
Everything created specifically for SafeSpace, once the engagement completes and the fee is paid in full: the hub's customized workflows and configuration, templates, dashboards, the fundraising strategy and materials, AI prompts written for SafeSpace, documentation, and reports. The hub itself holds strategy and goals, projects and tasks, a full CRM with donor and grant pipelines, and areas for program, finance, data, and compliance, with a board login SafeSpace controls. The fundraising collateral built together: a pitch deck, a send deck, and a one-pager. All data SafeSpace puts into the hub, or that its use generates, belongs to SafeSpace: exportable, along with this engagement record, in commonly used formats such as CSV or JSON at any time, whether or not support continues, and returned or deleted on request. The hub is a SafeSpace instance of BloomOS: Sobo Consulting keeps the platform and its codebase, along with its general methods, frameworks, and tools, and SafeSpace holds a perpetual, non-exclusive, royalty-free license to its instance and to any Sobo Consulting materials inside the delivered system, for its internal operations, surviving the end of the agreement. Remi stays available as a resource afterward, shape to be decided together.

### Hosting and what happens after
The first twelve months of hosting, maintenance, security updates, and support are included in the fee, starting the day the hub goes live. After that, SafeSpace can continue on a published BloomOS plan, month to month, cancel any time. Its plan is Bloom Grow, currently $250 a month, held at that rate for five years from the end of the included year. If SafeSpace chooses not to continue, it keeps its license and its data, and receives, at no cost, a full export in CSV or JSON with any stored documents and files, a written data dictionary explaining the export's structure, and up to 10 hours of transition help, with the export delivered within 30 days of a written request. If the platform is ever retired, SafeSpace gets at least 90 days' written notice. It is never locked in by its own data.

### Your data
SafeSpace owns and controls the data in the hub; Sobo Consulting processes it only to deliver this work. No identifiable youth information enters the hub until the two sides agree in writing, field by field, what will be stored and why, and SafeSpace confirms its parent or guardian consents cover that use; until then the hub holds organizational data only. Individual youth records are visible only to designated program staff, never to board members or fundraising-only roles, and identifiable youth information is never sent to or processed by AI models. Data is encrypted in transit and at rest, behind row-level access controls and named accounts with no shared logins.

### Roles
- **Remi** coaches the leadership and builds the system.
- **Dr. Kendra** designs the impact and evaluation framework and the funder-facing artifacts.
- **Shannon** holds the back office steady while the team learns to run it.
- **Susan** stays close to the day to day, strategizes on existing donors, sits in on selected sessions, and brings Aris and Jasmine onto real calls.
- **Liesl** moves toward an advisory seat. Her relationships are used deliberately: specific people assigned, letters drafted for her, made as easy as possible.
- **Aris and Jasmine** do the work: homework, reps, rhythms, and, increasingly, the calls. Execution is theirs, and it is the part no one can do for them.

### How we will know it worked
The signed agreement defines success in five parts, measured openly rather than assumed. The deliverables exist, built and handed over. Aris and Jasmine can teach it back: each can deliver the five-minute pitch unaided, present the fundraising strategy and the reasoning behind it to Susan and the board, and say why each part of the system exists. The system is documented and live in the hub, with the weekly, monthly, and quarterly rhythms named and owned and the pipelines current; the practical test is that a new person could pick it up and run the play. Execution is measured and reported as activities completed, never dollars raised: the weekly fundraising blocks held, the donor journeys worked, and each coachee through the full cycle of preparing for, leading, following up on, and rating a donor meeting. And confidence is tracked, not graded: Aris and Jasmine rate their own confidence across the core competencies at the start, monthly, and at the end, so growth is visible across the six months. Behind all five sit the three pillars checked honestly: **philosophy** (they understand how fundraising and nonprofit leadership actually work), **system** (a documented plan a professional fundraiser would respect), and **execution** (they are running it consistently, week to week). If any piece needs more time at six months, Remi stays on as a resource until it holds. Nothing gets dropped at the finish line.

### What this engagement is not
- It is not a quick fundraising sprint. The goal is capability that lasts, and more raised along the way is the expected byproduct, not the deliverable.
- The **Youth Action Board portal**, a **public website redesign**, and **ongoing support beyond the six months** are available as separate engagements, scoped on their own. They are named here so asking about them is easy and never awkward.
- **Bookkeeping, payroll, and transaction processing** stay with SafeSpace's bookkeeper; Shannon coordinates with the bookkeeper rather than duplicating that work. **Ongoing board governance consulting and board recruitment** are also not included.
- Program design stays with the program team. Fundraising touches every function, so we will talk about all of it, but this engagement does not run programs.

## 9. Resource library starters (SOBO IP, reusable beyond SafeSpace)

1. **Donor journeys:** stewardship and cultivation, both every week; the ask is never a surprise; quarterly touch cadence per donor.
2. **How to run a fundraising meeting:** prepare, execute, follow up, then debrief: likelihood, giving potential, possible ask.
3. **The weekly fundraising rhythm:** what a week looks like; planning the week around work blocks and priorities.
4. **Messaging angles:** the same true story told to different hearts: youth mental health; peer-led leadership development; community rootedness (the peninsula's own kids, grandparent and parent framings); accurate always, tuned per funder.
5. **Segmenting the base:** the 90/10 cut; custom strategies up top, letters and giving-tree below; program-cost framing for community fundraising, larger funders for the rest.
6. **Foundations versus individuals:** how institutional funders think, what they need, how the ask differs.
7. **Multi-year giving:** moving a yearly gift to a three-year commitment; when it is too early to push it (Susan's real experience: they tried once, too soon).
8. **AI in the daily workflow:** how to use it, how not to; it cleans up your thinking, you do the thinking; drafts outreach, never sends on its own.
9. **Positive framing:** no failure language, a learning organization; losses are lessons; never signal instability to donors.
